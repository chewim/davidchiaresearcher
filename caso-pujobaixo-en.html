Research / Pujobaixo

Pujobaixo

From the intention to carpool to actually posting a trip

Pujobaixo connects drivers and passengers making the trip between the Berguedà and Barcelona to split the cost of the journey (around €42 round trip). Posting a trip is free, comes with no commitment, and the deal is closed over WhatsApp. Almost everyone said they'd carpool; very few actually posted a trip.

01 Context

Almost everyone said they'd carpool between the Berguedà and Barcelona. Very few actually posted a trip.

Pujobaixo can be framed as research into the gap between a positive attitude —"I'd carpool between the Berguedà and Barcelona"— and the behavior the service actually needs to work: posting available seats. The regional context explains why this matters: the Berguedà is made up of scattered towns with mobility services suited to low-demand areas, while public authorities keep reinforcing public transport links to Barcelona instead. Pujobaixo answers that gap with a deliberately lightweight model: posting a trip is free and carries no commitment, and the deal — including the cost split, a reference of €5 per person on a roughly €42 round trip — is closed directly over WhatsApp, with no payments or bookings inside the app.

02 Team decision

Determine how to design the trip-posting flow so that a positive attitude toward carpooling would translate into the actual action of posting seats, without adding friction or asking the driver for a commitment they weren't ready to make yet.

03 Research question

What frictions, motivations and trust signals determine whether a driver posts a recurring trip?

04 Initial hypotheses

Financial savings spark interest, but aren't enough on their own to trigger a post. To take the step, the driver needs to feel:

  • Low effort: posting a trip has to feel quick and simple.
  • Control over the decision: they decide who rides with them, not the platform.
  • Real demand: that interested passengers are actually waiting, not an empty board.

05 Methodology & participants

The research combined semi-structured interviews with regular drivers on the route, a journey map of the current trip, a moderated usability test of the posting flow, and a short survey to prioritize the patterns detected in interviews.

06 Evidence observed

  • Interviews revealed trip habits, perceived costs, prior experiences sharing a vehicle, and fears related to strangers, the commitment to repeat, or scheduling coordination.
  • The journey map placed the biggest point of friction not in a lack of interest, but in the stretch between "I'd consider carpooling" and "getting the first reply" after posting.
  • The usability test showed that the value proposition, the posting flow itself, and selecting fellow travelers weren't always understood without help.
  • The savings (the €42 trip cost, up to €30 sharing three seats) generated interest once explained, but weren't, on their own, what led people to complete the post.

07 Patterns & insights

  • People don't post just because sharing is beneficial — they post when it feels simple, safe and useful from the very first trip.
  • Savings grab attention, but the driver needs to see the posting process itself as low-effort before completing it.
  • The feeling of control over who rides with them weighs as much as the financial savings when deciding.

08 Product implications

Closing the gap between intention and an actual post wasn't going to be solved by explaining the savings better: it required lowering the perceived effort of posting, giving the driver explicit control over their fellow travelers, and making both the loss of driving with empty seats and the real demand waiting on the other side tangible.

09 Decisions made

Decision Rationale
Visualize the money lost driving with empty seats (on the ~€42 trip) Turns an invisible loss into a tangible, concrete cost
Turn that loss into accumulated savings from sharing (up to €30 with three seats) Projected savings motivate more than the one-off cost of a single trip
Explain the posting process in three steps Reduces the perceived effort of posting a trip
Communicate that the driver decides who rides with them Gives real control over the decision, one of the main barriers

10 Outcome, limitations & next steps

The success of these changes is measured through understanding of the value proposition, the start and completion rate of the posting flow, time to complete the task, and contacts generated per posted trip.

Limitations: the interview sample focused on regular drivers already inclined to carpool, so it doesn't capture the barriers of people who rule out the idea from the start.

Next steps: measure the four success metrics after the posting-flow redesign, and check whether the usability test translates into a higher real completion rate.
