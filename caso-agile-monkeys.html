Agile Monkeys

The problem wasn't finding more records. It was paying less to receive them.

The research revealed that vinyl buyers didn't need more recommendations — they needed a trustworthy seller to bundle their order with and cut shipping costs.

01 Context

The team was exploring an AI-based solution, but we still didn't know what problem it needed to solve that would actually matter. The research let us question that initial hypothesis and find an opportunity grounded in real purchasing behavior.

Agile Monkeys was working with a vinyl retail client on an AI-based recommendation engine meant to help collectors discover new records. The technical approach was already defined, and the engineering team was getting ready to start development the following quarter.

02 Team decision

Determine whether the recommendation engine was worth the planned development investment, or whether there were more urgent, more relevant problems to solve before moving in that direction.

03 Research question

What do vinyl buyers actually need when deciding on and completing a purchase, and how much do personalized recommendations really influence that decision?

04 Initial hypotheses

  • Buyers give up on discovering new records due to a lack of relevant recommendations.
  • An AI-based recommendation system would increase purchase frequency.
  • Personalization is the main driver of loyalty in this type of store.

05 Methodology & participants

12 semi-structured interviews with regular vinyl buyers (3+ purchases per quarter), recruited by purchase frequency and channel (physical store and online). The fieldwork was complemented with desk research and thematic analysis of support conversations and specialist forums, to cross-check the patterns observed in interviews against spontaneous behavior.

06 Evidence observed

  • Most participants already discover music through labels, artists and communities they actively follow, not through recommendation engines.
  • 9 of 12 participants spontaneously mentioned shipping cost as a reason to delay, bundle or cancel a purchase.
  • They weren't looking to buy cheaper across several stores: they preferred finding one trustworthy seller to buy a batch of records from at once, rather than splitting the order among strangers to save a few cents.
  • Before buying, they check whether that store has enough records they're interested in to make a larger order worthwhile — not just whether the seller is trustworthy.
  • Several buyers use notes, lists, or deliberately abandoned carts as a manual way to "stack up" an order before paying for shipping.
"I want a trustworthy seller from whom I could buy a pack of vinyls without paying a lot for the shipping." Research insight, vinyl buyer
"I take into account the place where I am going to buy as I take the opportunity to make vinyl packs. I look for places where to buy that allow me to make more orders of things that interest me with the same shipping costs." Research insight, vinyl buyer

07 Patterns & insights

  • Music discovery is already a solved problem for the regular buyer, handled through social channels and external curation — they don't feel a gap there.
  • Shipping cost and friction are the real, unresolved barrier — users already bundle purchases manually, with no tools to make it easier.
  • Bundling an order only pays off if the seller inspires trust: the shipping savings don't justify the risk of buying a large batch from a stranger.
  • The decision of where to buy isn't just "I trust this seller" — it's "this store has enough of a catalog I'm interested in to make ordering several records at once worth it."
  • Trust in an "algorithmic" selection is low compared to the human curation of labels, stores and communities.

08 Product implications

Investing in recommendations wasn't going to solve the real purchasing friction: the problem was in the ordering and shipping process, not in product discovery. The product opportunity wasn't just about bundling orders or showing trust signals — it was about helping buyers find sellers whose catalog covered several records they wanted at once. Without that, neither trust nor shipping savings were enough of an incentive to bundle an order.

09 Decisions made

Decision Rationale
Pause development of the AI recommendation engine It didn't address the purchasing barrier identified in the research
Re-prioritize the roadmap toward order bundling Responds to an existing, unresolved behavior
Show seller trust signals (ratings, shipping history) alongside the order-bundling option Bundling only pays off if the buyer trusts the seller
Explore how to flag which sellers carry several records from the buyer's wantlist or interests, to make bundling easier Buyers are already doing this catalog-matching by hand, store by store
Define a concept test before committing to development Validate intent to use before investing in engineering

10 Outcome, limitations & next steps

The evidence made it possible to rethink the product's initial direction before building a solution with little real relevance, saving weeks of development on a feature with low expected impact.

Limitations: the sample focused on frequent buyers, so it doesn't represent occasional or gift buyers — a segment where discovery might actually carry more weight.

Next steps: validate two order-bundling proposals through concept testing and measure intent to use before committing engineering resources.
