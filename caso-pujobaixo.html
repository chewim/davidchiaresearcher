Pujobaixo

From a WhatsApp group to a local network for sharing rides

Pujobaixo grew out of a community that already shared rides between the Berguedà and Barcelona. The challenge wasn't convincing people to carpool, but turning informal coordination into a system able to handle more supply and demand without losing its simplicity.

01 Context

Before turning the idea into a product, we created a WhatsApp group with community members who frequently shared their trips between the Berguedà and Barcelona.

Posts were simple: they usually stated the day, the origin, and the direction of the trip. Interested people finished coordinating directly and split the trip's costs, with no fixed rates or formal payment system.

This system worked within a small community, but it started to show limitations as the number of messages and coordination needs grew.

Pujobaixo's goal was to make better use of vehicles that were already making the trip, reduce trips with empty seats, and build a local network with enough supply and demand to enable matches.

02 Decision the team had to make

We had to determine how to move an informal WhatsApp dynamic into a digital product without adding unnecessary processes or removing the flexibility that made the community work.

The decision wasn't just about designing an interface. We needed to identify:

  • What information was essential to find a match.
  • What parts of the coordination could be structured.
  • What interactions needed to stay open and flexible.
  • How to make available supply and demand visible.

03 Research question

How can we turn the informal coordination of a community that shares rides into a scalable network, keeping it simple and reducing the effort needed to find a match?

As a secondary question, we examined which elements of the existing behavior we needed to preserve, so as not to turn a flexible practice into an overly rigid process.

04 Initial hypotheses

  • There was enough recurring trip volume to build a local network.
  • Centralizing active trips would make it easier to find matches.
  • Structuring information would reduce the conversations needed to coordinate each trip.
  • Final coordination needed to stay on a familiar channel like WhatsApp.
  • Savings could reinforce the value proposition, though they weren't the only motivation to carpool.

05 Methodology and participants

The research started from observing a WhatsApp group made up of community members who already regularly shared their trips.

Instead of only asking whether they would use the service, we analyzed behavior that was already happening:

  • What information they included when offering or requesting a trip.
  • How interested people responded.
  • At what points coordination moved to private messages.
  • What difficulties came up when searching for past trips.
  • How supply and demand varied by day and direction.
  • What made it hard to identify an available match.

These findings were progressively carried over into the flow design and the build of the first working product.

06 Evidence observed

The group confirmed there was a community willing to share rides and split trip costs.

However, a chronological conversation didn't work as a management system:

  • It was hard to tell which trips were still available.
  • Necessary information wasn't always complete.
  • Older messages got buried under new conversations.
  • Finding a match depended on manually scrolling through the group.
  • There was no combined view of supply and demand.
  • Coordination got harder as activity increased.

The group solved communication between people, but it didn't allow the network to be organized or scaled.

07 Patterns and insights

1. The behavior already existed

We didn't need to create the carpooling habit from scratch. The community was already doing it whenever it found a suitable match.

2. Coordination was the main friction

The problem wasn't only a lack of interest, but the effort needed to locate a compatible trip, check whether it was still available, and complete the missing information.

3. Scaling required structuring, not complicating

The product had to organize trips without turning a simple practice into a rigid or bureaucratic process.

4. The network's visibility shaped its usefulness

For Pujobaixo to generate value, drivers and passengers needed to see active supply and demand in each direction of the trip.

5. WhatsApp still served a useful function

The platform could organize discovery and enable matching, while WhatsApp let people keep a final coordination step that was direct, flexible, and familiar.

08 Product implications

The research showed that Pujobaixo shouldn't fully replace the existing behavior, but structure the parts that generated the most effort.

The main opportunity was centralizing active trips, standardizing the necessary information, and clearly distinguishing between those offering seats and those looking for a ride.

It was also important to keep a lightweight model: the platform would enable the match, but people would keep control over the decision to travel together and over final coordination.

Savings had to be communicated as a tangible benefit of using empty seats, without reducing the value proposition to a financial transaction. The main purpose remained optimizing trips and strengthening a local shared-mobility network.

09 Decisions made

Separating supply and demand

We organized trips into two categories: Offering and Needing. This made it easy to quickly distinguish between drivers with available seats and people looking for a ride.

Structuring trip information

Each post grouped together the data needed to evaluate a potential match: origin, destination, date, time, seats, and direction of the route.

Centralizing active trips

Trips stopped depending on the chronological order of a conversation and started appearing as searchable, up-to-date posts.

Keeping contact on WhatsApp

Once a match was found, people could contact each other directly to finish agreeing on the details. This narrowed the product's initial scope and preserved a dynamic the community already knew.

Giving users control over their posts

Users could check, manage, or cancel their own trips, making it more visible which offers were actually still available.

Making the shared benefit visible

The product's messaging incorporated the savings from splitting costs, along with the collective benefit of reducing vehicles with empty seats.

The new flow was structured into a simple sequence: post a need or offer → find an active match → coordinate the trip on WhatsApp.

10 Outcome, limitations, and next steps

Pujobaixo turned scattered coordination into a working product able to centralize supply and demand for trips between the Berguedà and Barcelona.

The research's main contribution was identifying that the value wasn't in inventing a new behavior, but in reducing the coordination cost of a practice that already existed.

The product made it possible to formalize the minimum information, make active trips visible, and prepare the dynamic to bring in a wider community.

Limitations: the initial observation focused on people who were already predisposed to carpool and belonged to one specific community. This helped us understand the behavior of active users, but it doesn't yet explain:

  • The barriers of people who have never carpooled.
  • Trust needs between strangers.
  • How the network behaves with a larger volume of users.
  • The balance needed between supply and demand to generate recurring matches.

Next steps: the next phase should analyze the product's real behavior through:

  • Number of trips offered and requested.
  • Matches generated by direction and time slot.
  • Time elapsed until first contact.
  • Percentage of posts that receive a response.
  • Trips canceled or no longer available.
  • Users who post again.
  • Ratio between active drivers and passengers.

Evidence → Insight → Decision

Evidence

The community already shared trips, but it was hard to know which ones were still available and to find a match within the group.

→
Insight

The main barrier wasn't a lack of willingness to carpool, but the effort of coordinating supply and demand through an unstructured conversation.

→
Decision

Centralize active trips, separate supply and demand, structure the necessary information, and keep WhatsApp for final coordination.

Key takeaway: scaling a community practice doesn't always mean replacing it. Sometimes it means identifying what already works, structuring only the friction points, and preserving the flexibility that made the original behavior possible.
