Pujobaixo

From a WhatsApp experiment to a scalable local network

Pujobaixo started as a field experiment: we created a WhatsApp group with people who regularly made the trip between the Berguedà and Barcelona, to observe how supply, demand and matches showed up in a real context.

The challenge was turning those learnings into a network able to handle more trips without losing the simplicity and flexibility of the original coordination.

01 Context and opportunity

The habit of sharing rides already existed within the community. Some people used their regular trips between the Berguedà and Barcelona to share available seats and split the cost of the journey.

However, we had no structured information about how often these trips happened, how supply and demand related to each other, or what conditions made a match more likely.

Before building a platform, we needed to observe how this dynamic actually worked.

We created a WhatsApp group with people who made the trip frequently. The group let members offer or request rides by sharing basic information such as the day, the origin, and the direction of the trip. Interested people finished coordinating directly.

The goal wasn't to introduce a new behavior, but to understand an existing practice and study how it could become a more useful, scalable local network.

02 Decision we had to make

We had to decide whether informal coordination through WhatsApp could evolve into a digital product able to serve a wider community.

To do that, we needed to understand:

  • What information was essential to find a match.
  • How supply and demand actually showed up.
  • Which parts of the coordination created the most effort.
  • What dynamics we needed to preserve.
  • What elements needed a specific structure.
  • Whether there was enough recurrence to justify a local network.

The decision wasn't just about designing an interface. First, we needed to understand what system was already emerging within the community.

03 Research question

How can we turn informal coordination of shared rides into a scalable network, keeping it simple and reducing the effort needed to find a match?

As secondary questions, we examined:

  • What information do drivers and passengers share spontaneously?
  • What makes it hard to know if a trip is still available?
  • How do supply and demand fluctuate?
  • What should the product solve, and what can stay in direct conversation?
  • What conditions make a match more likely to appear?

04 Initial hypotheses

We started from five hypotheses:

  • There was enough recurring trip volume to build a local network.
  • Centralizing active trips would make it easier to find matches.
  • Structuring information would reduce the effort of coordination.
  • Final communication needed to stay on a familiar, flexible channel.
  • Sharing costs could reinforce the value proposition, though it wasn't the only motivation to carpool.

The WhatsApp group let us test these hypotheses against real behavior, not only stated opinions.

05 Experiment and methodology

We used the WhatsApp group as a field experiment and a low-fidelity service prototype.

Instead of only asking whether people would use a shared-mobility platform, we created an environment where coordination could happen and observed how its members behaved.

We analyzed:

  • How often trips were offered or requested.
  • What information people posted spontaneously.
  • How they expressed origin, destination, and day of travel.
  • How matches showed up.
  • What information was missing to make a decision.
  • At what point coordination moved to private messages.
  • What difficulties came up as activity increased.
  • How supply and demand varied by day and direction.

The experiment let us study the dynamic before investing in a more complex product.

06 Evidence observed

The group confirmed there were people willing to share their trips and split costs when they found a suitable match.

It also revealed the limits of using a chronological conversation as a coordination system:

  • It was hard to tell which trips were still available.
  • Information showed up unevenly.
  • Some posts didn't include all the necessary data.
  • Earlier trips got buried under new messages.
  • Finding a match required manually scrolling through the conversation.
  • There was no combined view of supply and demand.
  • As activity increased, coordination lost clarity.

WhatsApp made contact between people easy, but it didn't allow the network to be organized at a larger scale.

07 Patterns and insights

1. The habit existed, but the network didn't — yet

People were already willing to share trips when they found a suitable match.

The opportunity wasn't creating that behavior, but connecting it within a more visible, recurring and scalable system.

2. The main friction was finding the match

The problem wasn't only a lack of supply or demand.

The difficulty was in locating a compatible trip, checking whether it was still active, and completing the information needed to coordinate it.

3. WhatsApp validated the dynamic, but couldn't scale it

The group worked as the service's first prototype and let us observe real behavior.

However, the conversation's chronological structure wasn't suited to managing more users, trips, and simultaneous matches.

4. Scaling required structuring, not complicating

The product had to organize information and reduce search effort without turning a flexible practice into a rigid process.

5. The network's visibility shaped its usefulness

A shared-mobility network needs drivers and passengers to quickly identify what supply and demand exist in each direction and moment.

08 Product implications

The research showed that Pujobaixo shouldn't fully replace the existing dynamic, but structure the parts that generated the most effort.

The main opportunity was centralizing active trips, standardizing the necessary information, and clearly distinguishing between those offering seats and those looking for a ride.

The platform had to make discovering matches easier, but people needed to keep control over who they traveled with and how they finished coordinating.

We also needed to keep a lightweight model. There was no need to build a closed booking, payment, or automatic-assignment system from the start.

Pujobaixo had to act as an organizing layer over a behavior that already worked:

  • Order supply and demand.
  • Make active trips visible.
  • Reduce manual searching.
  • Make contact easier.
  • Prepare the network to bring in more users and trips.

Savings were incorporated as a tangible benefit of using empty seats, without reducing the value proposition to a financial transaction.

09 Decisions made

Separating supply and demand

We organized trips into two categories:

  • Offering, for drivers with available seats.
  • Needing, for people looking for a ride.

This separation made it easy to quickly identify what type of post the user was looking at.

Structuring trip information

Each post grouped together the data needed to evaluate a potential match:

  • Origin.
  • Destination.
  • Date.
  • Time.
  • Direction of the route.
  • Number of seats.
  • Other relevant information.

This reduced the need to fill in data across several conversations.

Centralizing active trips

Trips stopped depending on the chronological order of a group and started appearing as searchable posts.

The network could offer a clearer view of which trips were available at any given moment.

Keeping WhatsApp for final coordination

Once a match was found, people could contact each other directly through WhatsApp.

This let us narrow the product's initial scope and preserve a channel the community already knew.

Giving users control over availability

Users could manage and cancel their own trips.

This decision helped keep information up to date and reduced uncertainty about which posts were still active.

Preparing the solution to scale

The platform turned a manually managed experiment into a digital network able to bring in more drivers, passengers, and trips.

The main flow was reduced to a simple sequence: post an offer or need → find an active match → coordinate the trip directly.

10 Outcome, limitations, and next steps

Pujobaixo turned a WhatsApp coordination experiment into a working product able to centralize supply and demand for trips between the Berguedà and Barcelona.

The research's main contribution was showing that the value wasn't in inventing a new behavior, but in reducing the coordination cost of a practice that already existed.

The product made it possible to:

  • Structure each trip's minimum information.
  • Differentiate supply and demand.
  • Make active trips visible.
  • Make it easier to search for matches.
  • Keep a direct final coordination step.
  • Prepare the dynamic to reach a wider community.

Limitations: the experiment ran within one specific community, with people who already showed some predisposition to carpool. This phase helped us understand active users, but it still doesn't fully explain:

  • The barriers of people who have never carpooled.
  • Trust needs between strangers.
  • How the network behaves with a larger volume of users.
  • The minimum balance needed between supply and demand.
  • How many matches actually turned into completed trips.

Next steps: the next phase should evaluate the network's real behavior through:

  • Number of trips offered and requested.
  • Matches generated by direction and time slot.
  • Percentage of posts that receive a response.
  • Time to first contact.
  • Trips canceled or no longer available.
  • Users who post again.
  • Ratio between active drivers and passengers.
  • Trips that end up actually happening.
  • Estimated savings per vehicle and per user.
