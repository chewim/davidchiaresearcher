01 Context

We started out assuming age was the main barrier. The research showed the real problem was a lack of familiar reference points, guidance and control.

Triportation is a virtual reality product by The Wise Dreams, built to bring immersive travel experiences to a broad audience. When reviewing actual usage of the app, the team noticed that a large share of users aged 40 and up were abandoning the experience within the first few minutes. The first read was a simple one: "they're older, technology is hard for them." The real research challenge wasn't just checking whether the interface made sense — it went deeper than that: how do you introduce a still-unfamiliar technology to a specific audience without designing from that assumption?

02 Team decision

Decide whether the problem could be solved with a longer tutorial, explicitly built "for older users," or whether the team needed to research what was actually driving the drop-off before designing anything around age as a variable.

03 Research question

What barriers explain why some people abandon Triportation within the first few minutes, and how much does age really matter compared to other behavioral factors?

04 Initial hypotheses

  • Age is the main barrier: the older someone is, the harder it is for them to navigate in VR.
  • A longer tutorial, designed for older beginners, would resolve most of the difficulties.
  • Physical discomfort (motion sickness, disorientation) is the main cause, regardless of user profile.

05 Methodology & participants

To avoid designing from an age-based stereotype, participants were segmented by behavior rather than age alone: prior tech experience, travel frequency, familiarity with VR, and perceived need for guidance. The research combined an exploratory phase —interviews to uncover barriers— with an evaluative phase —moderated usability tests to validate solutions— using cross-cutting profiles: people 40+ with both high and low tech familiarity, as well as younger participants with little prior experience in gaming or VR.

06 Evidence observed

  • Within the 40+ group, people who traveled frequently and already used booking or map apps oriented themselves in the virtual environment almost as well as younger participants.
  • The difficulty wasn't concentrated by age: people who had never used a game controller didn't understand what each button did, regardless of how old they were.
  • Explicit emotional responses came up —fear of "doing it wrong," a feeling of losing control— in both older participants and at least one younger participant with no prior VR experience.
  • The test environment itself added complexity beyond learning: physically handling the controllers, spatial orientation, fatigue after a few minutes, and motion sickness.
  • Several participants asked to feel accompanied during their first session, not simply to have more explanatory text in the tutorial.
"I didn't know where I'd ended up. Every time I pressed the button I was scared of getting lost." Participant, 58, first VR experience

07 Patterns & insights

  • Age wasn't the determining factor: tech familiarity and travel frequency better explained who adapted quickly and who didn't.
  • The real problem was a lack of familiar reference points —not recognizing control conventions— not a lower capacity to learn something new.
  • Feeling accompanied and in control of what was about to happen reduced the fear of "doing it wrong" more than any extra explanation in the tutorial.

08 Product implications

Designing a tutorial "for older users" would have started from the wrong stereotype and left the real barrier unresolved, since it was also present in younger users with no VR experience. The product opportunity wasn't about the user's age — it was about providing familiar reference points, progressive guidance and a sense of control from the very first minute, with direct implications for accessibility and technology adoption: two areas that carry a lot of strategic weight for a product still new to the market.

09 Decisions made

Decision Rationale
Replace the single tutorial with progressive onboarding, adapted to detected tech familiarity rather than age The real barrier was a lack of familiar reference points, not the user's age
Add guided navigation and simplified controls to the first session Reduces the fear of "doing it wrong" and the feeling of losing control
Design a transition from web or mobile into VR, with familiar reference points before putting on the headset Provides guidance and context before entering a completely unfamiliar environment

10 Outcome, limitations & next steps

Segmenting by behavior instead of age changed the direction of the redesign: the team moved from planning a tutorial "for older users" to implementing progressive onboarding and a guided web/mobile-to-VR transition. My collaboration with The Wise Dreams ended in May 2026, before the full quantitative outcome of the redesign was available.

Limitations: the cross-profile sample was small; fully separating emotional factors (fear, control) from purely motor ones (handling the controllers) is difficult with so few participants.

Next steps (recommended to the team): validate the progressive onboarding with a larger, behavior-segmented sample, and measure whether the web/app-to-VR transition reduces early drop-off among users with low tech familiarity, regardless of age.
